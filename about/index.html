I review literature, analyse data, and write up reports on population health, international development, and climate change policies and interventions at an effective altruist research organization that’s part–think tank (with its main audience being readers of the EA Forum and sometimes academia) and part-consultancy (with clients including the folks at Open Philanthropy). I also copyedit Asterisk magazine, a quarterly about existential risk published by the Centre for Effective Altruism.

Effective altruism is a research field and social movement that tries to figure out the best ways to do good. We ask big questions and think we have preliminary answers to some of them. Some people focus on the long-term future; others focus on present-day human and non-human suffering. Some people think it’s exciting that there’s so much good to be done; others think it’s a moral obligation to maximize utils or utilons (for the record, I’m on the “excited” end of the spectrum). If this sounds interesting, maybe check out 80,000 Hours, an Oxford-based organization that offers free career advice.

I majored in environmental science at the University of Chicago. Environmental science is housed in the geosciences department at UChicago, so I got to do cool stuff like spend time researching lead pollution at the Ecosystems Center of the Marine Biological Laboratory and do my optional undergraduate thesis on the history of Mars’s orbital obliquity with the department's planetary geoscience group. I also did some history, some computer science, and some classical Chinese, in addition to the Core Curriculum. I went to an American college because I felt it was important to get a liberal arts education.

I was born in London, England, to Chinese academics. My mum’s an academic psychiatrist who studies schizophrenia, and I don’t really know what my dad researches but I do know he’s a chemist-turned-physicist-turned-biologist. I was raised by my waipo or maternal grandma (1942–2022), who lived through the Japanese air raids on Chungking, lived with us for as long as I could remember.

I remember spending a lot of my time on my own rewatching BBC science and nature documentaries, rereading Roald Dahl’s children’s corpus, and flipping forwards and backwards through a peeling-at-the-edges, formerly bright purple copy of the Usborne Children’s Encyclopedia. My family moved back to China in 2007, where I had most of my formative experiences. We lived in Chengdu, a cloudy city of around 10 million inhabitants at the western edge of the Sichuan basin, where I went to international school. I was into journalism in high school, where I founded and edited a bilingual student magazine for three years and briefly dreamed of being a magazine editor when I grew up.

As of 2022, I live in London with my partner Puyu, who’s doing an MSc in clinical mental health sciences at UCL. I like national parks, board games, and spicy food. I use en-GB-oxendict (which means I write “analyse” but “synthesize”, prefer en-dashes spaced – like so – and punctuation marks “untucked”, and always use the serial comma) and can appreciate a good haiku. I’ve also pledged to donate 10 per cent of my income to the most effective charities for the rest of my life or until I retire.
